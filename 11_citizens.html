Citizens

Citizens was a sermon series was based on 1 Peter's concept of "Christian citizenship"—one rooted in the "living hope" of Christ, that promised imperishable spiritual reward to a church alienated and oppressed in its Roman-empire context.

Drawing inspiration from the US citizenship application and artists like Jenny Holzer, I developed a system of digital brushes and graphics that reproduce elements of the USCIS N-400 form (first form in the US Citizenship process). By turning these formal elements into expressive marks, the final designs connote the organic order that characterizes spiritual citizenship—one qualified by Divine Authority and living hope, rather than bureacracy and due process.

《 Weekly Covers 》



Each week's cover slide contained similar visual elements: "Citizens" as the series title, a page number that changed each week, several "form" element-marks, and the sermon title written cursive (the line borrowed from proportions of the N-400's' divider lines. Background colors were inspired by the pastels tones of office paper. I've put the five cover slides I designed below.


As part of a weekly process, these compositions would be made in addition to several social media assets and an animated video bumper which preceded sermon recordings on the church's Youtube channel. I worked with fellow Design Team member Jessie Haedrick to make the weekly assets, and also created a companion sermon template for the teaching staff at church.


Later versions of this bumper evolved into a more complex animation I developed with Creative Team Leader (and former animator) Christen Smith. We added some real dust and scratches to give more of a scanned feeling to the animation, and added a line draw to emphasize the hand-drawn nature of the text.

《 Inspiration: Holzer and "Government Formal" 》

One of my first meaningful encounters with Jenny Holzer's work was at an exhibition at Mass MOCA in West Adams, MA. A room of the rotating exhibit at the time featured oil-painting reproductions of declassified government documents, including censored handprints, frank approvals of torture techniques, and powerpoint slides which had been shown to President Bush. It had been Holzer's hope that her platform and process as an artist might bring some of these hidden documents into spotlight.


Holzer takes little artistic license with these reproductions, knowing their power is in her faithfulness to each and every detail. The "undesign" of government documents is often the subject of parody, yet here its exact visual language lends a kind of sinister credibility. In other words, it is exactly the formal appearance of each document that makes these paintings strong. This visual style has become associated with the American government's authority and power.

Christians believe in a form of government higher still, ordered in the heaven by divine purpose and appointment. To appropriate the trappings of an earthly government gestures towards the basis of Spiritual Citizenship—a government with authority on power "on the shoulders" of God the Son.

｢ Appropriating Formality ｣



The "government style" is not all that hard to dissect. U.S. passports are clad in navy linen, with classic serif faces and a golden seal. Most government forms use a crowd-pleasing Times New Roman and are packed full of text, lines, boxes, and numbering systems.

As I did some more research on citizenship on the US, I quickly landed on the USCIS N-400 form as basis for visual exploration. I set about replicating various elements in Illustrator, and experimenting with different ways of imitating the government style. Since I didn't want to aligned it too closely with the government, I decided to take out references like various seals, and opted for a simplified version of the form.

｢ Living Citizenship ｣



1 Peter's describes the basis of Christian citizenship as "living hope," Christians themselves as "living stones," and these stones as chosen and precious to God. For all of its power and authority, Spiritual Citizenship is by all accounts of a different nature in everything but name. How would one show this difference?

As I continued to experiment I discovered art brushes, a nifty function of Illustrator that lets anyone create a custom brush with some drag and drop. By converting the N-400 form into a brush, the forms can now "come alive," freed from vector format into something richer and ripe for all kinds of experimentation.

There was something primal feeling about the animation of these forms. To take something so strongly associated with bureacracy and boredom and transform it into lively, personable marks felt like a small-scale resurrection. As inspiration, I began examining inspirations from children's handwriting and rudimentary tools. At RISD, we'd constantly been taught the value of markmaking and mark-making tools as integral to the character of writing. The primacy of a legible-but-not-perfect cursive felt like a refreshing, living contrast to the more stolid character of Time New Roman and other graphic elements.


The final system is based on a simplified version of the N-400 form, with most elements converted to brushes and a few more conventional graphic elements. Each week, a new composition wasbe composed by hand, encouraging an inconsistent and human touch to each.

《 Closing Thoughts 》

The concept for this series seemed to fall togetgher pretty quickly. The flow from Citizenship to Holzer/Government forms to art brushes came together in a few hours of desk research, but it was actually the experimentation of brushes, compositions, and layouts that seemed most finnicky. I thought handwriting was pretty intuitive, but spent many hours drawing and redrawing rs interesting enough, but not too illegible

This was one of the first projects that I animated each week. It was terrifying as a relative beginner in After Effects, but formed some of the foundation for later, motion-focused projects like Unexpected Jesus.

The most satisfying part was maybe that initial, hours-long flash of inspiration that came. I feel like it doesn't usually happen that easily, but this was one of the rare cases where the first idea was more or less fit for the job. As a project, it's not often one can play with intentionally boring design, and I was proud of how I managed to find a final process that felt so clearly referential without losing freshness.
