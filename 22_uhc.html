UHC E&I Sales

UHC provides healthcare coverage to roughly 1 in 3 Americans, and its sales and account teams are the frontline of this service. Facing pressure from an increasingly dynamic market, SYPartners was hired by UHC’s Employer & Individual (E&I) business to help them better support UHC’s mission by enhancing their sales culture.

《 Project Context 》

With a new AT&T Purpose codified over the end of 2021, SYPartners was asked by Chief Marketing and Growth Officer Kellyn Smith Kenny and CEO, John Stankey to develop a series of innovative new products/offerings. These offerings were meant to bring AT&T's new purpose to life while helping drive drive revenue growth, customer/employee retention, and overall greater efficiency within the organization.

SYP and AT&T gathered five cross-functional teams from across the enterprise to participate in a multi-week series of "sprints" which included virtual speaker sessions and workshops, user interviews, an in-person prototyping session, and preliminary prototype testing. The work culminated in a series of video "pitches" filmed and reviewed by external and internal advisors. Selected projects would be awarded, celebrated, and earmarked for resourcing and rollout within the company.

《 Visual Context 》

AT&T is an old company, and a lot of the challenges we encountered were based around building belief rather than necessarily time or resources. Time and time again, we heard warnings that the ideas might not be great, or that AT&T employees were "generally uncreative." Good ideas were far and few between, risk appetite was low, and many employees didn't feel safe to brainstorm and toss ideas at the wall in an environment where every idea needed to be backed up with exhaustive research and robust financial modelling. Further than this, creativity itself was a mode of working which seemed entirely foreign to many of our participants, who were nominated from around the organization. Many thus entered the process with a little past experience and more than a little trepidation around whether their boss (and CEO) would approve of their performance.

The sum total of these influences meant that every aspect of the Sprints would need to be designed to signal creative looseness and safety in a marked departure from stodgy business-as-usual. We wantede to move away from the typical environment of AT&T's internal, tightly-aligned corporate style, to something that felt unrefined, organic, and imperfect in reflection of the true creative process.

《 Concept: Blind Contour Drawings 》

The solution came in concert with some of the experience design we were doing around the first sprint. As we realized many of these teammates had never met, we needed to plan a short and efficient exercise for introductions. We quickly turned back to one of SYP's classic intro exercises: doing blind contour drawings of a partner where you don't look at the page while you draw, and try to do the full drawing in a single line. In drawing class, this is meant as an exercise in observation and economy of line; in a corporate context, we found it a wonderful way to familiarize yourself with a new face, while encouraging a fun and imperfect mode of creation.

As we thought more about it we realized that this initial exercise of blind contour drawing could be likened to the Sprint process itself. In a moment of immense change, facing a bevy of internal and external pressures, AT&T was looking to do something future-facing with little visibility ahead. Just like contour drawings—meant to be cheap and gestural creations—the signatures themselves were meant to be quick and fast "sketches" of new, company-defining work.


Initial compositions (above) were a bit looser, with a bit of an abstract composition. As we considered scale and speed, some of the higher-work ideas were abandoned in favor of styles that allowed for quick and efficient composition. Thus later iterations used solid shapes with bright, off-primary colors.


The final direction took a little more care with each illustration, while still using basic shapes and colors (at client request) more aligned to AT&T Brand. Each shape also functioned as a color-identity for its corresponding sprint, meaning the sketches could be used both as cover illustration and icon.

Execution of all final deliverables stemmed from this exploration, using only elements of sketch, type, and colored shape.
